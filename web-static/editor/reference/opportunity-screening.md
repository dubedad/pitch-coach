# Reference — how investors screen an opportunity

A pitch deck is a founder's *argument* that a real opportunity exists. This file is
the rubric investors use, consciously or not, to test that argument. Use it to judge
whether the deck is defending a genuinely fundable opportunity — and to name, in
investor terms, where the opportunity itself (not just the slide) is weak.

Every criterion below sits on a spectrum from **higher potential** to **lower
potential.** You're not scoring the deck like a spreadsheet; you're spotting which
signals land on the "lower potential" side and surfacing them as questions.

## The screen (higher-potential vs. lower-potential signals)

### Industry & market
| Criterion | Higher potential | Lower potential |
|---|---|---|
| Market need | Clear, urgent, observable | Vague, "nice to have" |
| Customers | Reachable, identifiable, payers | Diffuse "everyone," non-payers |
| User benefit | Large, obvious, measurable | Marginal, hard to feel |
| Value added | High, defensible | Low, easily matched |
| Market structure | Fragmented / imperfect / winnable | Concentrated / locked up |
| Market size | Big enough for a venture outcome | Too small to matter |
| Growth rate | Fast, expanding | Flat or shrinking |
| Attainable share | Credible path to meaningful share | No plausible wedge |

### Economics
| Criterion | Higher potential | Lower potential |
|---|---|---|
| Gross margins | High, durable | Thin, eroding |
| Time to breakeven | Short, plausible | Distant, hand-waved |
| Capital required | Modest to reach proof | Heavy before any signal |
| Unit economics | Work at the unit level | Only work "at scale, someday" |

### Competitive advantage
| Criterion | Higher potential | Lower potential |
|---|---|---|
| Barriers to entry | Real, building | None; anyone can copy |
| Proprietary protection | Something owned/hard to replicate | Nothing defensible |
| Lead / response time | Ahead, hard to catch | Easily overtaken |
| Contacts & networks | Unfair, relevant access | Generic |
| Control over costs/price/distribution | Meaningful leverage | At the mercy of others |

### Team
| Criterion | Higher potential | Lower potential |
|---|---|---|
| Founder-market fit | Lived the problem, shipped before | Enthusiasm only |
| Industry / technical depth | Deep, relevant | Thin, borrowed |
| Completeness | Key roles covered | Obvious gaps ("CEO: TBD") |

### Harvest / exit
| Criterion | Higher potential | Lower potential |
|---|---|---|
| Value-added potential | Clear acquirers / outcome | No obvious path to value |
| Exit mechanism | Plausible harvest story | None considered |

## Fatal-flaw and risk framing

Beyond the criteria, investors look for the thing that sinks the whole venture:

- **Fatal-flaw check** — is there one assumption that, if false, kills it? Name it
  and ask whether the deck addresses it.
- **Risks vs. rewards, each with likelihood** — a credible deck names its major
  risks *and* their likelihood, not just upside. A deck that shows only reward reads
  as naïve; an investor will supply the risks you omitted, uncharitably.
- **"And then a miracle happens"** — any step where the plan jumps from traction to
  dominance with no mechanism. Flag the gap.

## How to use it when critiquing

- Translate a weak slide into the *screening criterion it fails.* ("This isn't just
  a thin market slide — it signals a **small, slow market**, which is the first
  thing an investor screens out.")
- When the opportunity itself looks lower-potential, say so honestly and locate it on
  the screen — don't pretend slide polish will fix a structural problem.
- Turn each weak signal into a **question the founder must answer**, never a fix you
  supply. You're stress-testing their opportunity, not redesigning it.
