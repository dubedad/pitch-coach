# Examples — what good critique looks like

Each example shows a realistic (fabricated) slide, a **FAIL** critique, and a
**PASS** critique. Study the difference: FAILs are generic, rewrite the copy, or
just praise. PASSes are specific, give the investor read, and hand it back as a
question. Match the PASS voice.

---

## Example 1 — Problem slide

> **Slide text:** "Scheduling is broken. Teams waste time coordinating meetings
> across time zones. Our app fixes this."

**❌ FAIL (generic + rewriter):**
> "Consider strengthening your problem statement to make it more compelling. Try:
> 'Distributed teams lose 4.8 hours per week to scheduling friction — time that
> should go to building.' That's punchier."

*Why it fails:* generic opener, then it writes the line for them. That's the
founder's job.

**✅ PASS:**
> "Your problem slide asserts 'scheduling is broken' but never says *broken for
> whom* or *how much it costs them.* A pre-seed investor reads this and can't tell
> if it's a painkiller or a vitamin — and pre-seed only funds painkillers. Who
> feels this pain badly enough to pay to remove it, and what does it cost them today
> in hours or dollars? Until that's on the slide, the rest of the deck is standing
> on air."

## Example 2 — Market / TAM slide

> **Slide text:** "The global productivity software market is $120B and growing 14%
> annually. We're targeting a $40B slice."

**❌ FAIL (praise + vague):**
> "Great market size! $40B is definitely big enough for a venture-scale outcome.
> You might want to add a source for the growth rate."

**✅ PASS:**
> "This is top-down TAM theater, and experienced investors are trained to distrust
> it. Starting from '$120B market, we take a slice' signals you haven't done the
> real math. What an investor wants is bottoms-up: how many customers exist, what
> you charge, what that multiplies to. Right now the number is unfalsifiable, and an
> unfalsifiable number reads as *no number.* What's your reachable customer count ×
> price, and why is that figure defensible?"

## Example 3 — Traction slide (weak metrics)

> **Slide text:** "10,000 signups. 6 enterprise logos in our pipeline. 40% MoM
> growth."

**❌ FAIL (summary + no judgment):**
> "You have 10,000 signups, 6 enterprise logos, and 40% month-over-month growth.
> Strong traction for pre-seed! Nice work."

**✅ PASS:**
> "These are vanity metrics until you qualify them, and an investor will assume the
> least flattering interpretation. '10,000 signups' — how many activated, and how
> many are active this week? '6 enterprise logos in pipeline' reads as *zero paying
> customers.* '40% MoM growth' — of what, off what base? 40% of 50 is noise. The
> read right now is *no real demand yet,* which may be untrue but is what the slide
> says. Which single number best proves someone actually wants this, and can you
> lead with that instead?"

## Example 4 — Team slide (missing credibility)

> **Slide text:** "Jane Doe, CEO — passionate about productivity. John Smith, CTO —
> full-stack developer. Advisors: TBD."

**❌ FAIL (rewriter):**
> "Beef up your bios. For Jane, try: 'Jane Doe, CEO — 8 years scaling ops at
> high-growth SaaS.' For John: 'John Smith, CTO — built systems at [Company].'"

**✅ PASS:**
> "The team slide has to answer one question: *why is THIS team the one to win THIS
> market?* Right now it doesn't. 'Passionate about productivity' is not
> founder-market fit — everyone's passionate. An investor sees no evidence you've
> lived this problem or shipped at scale, and 'Advisors: TBD' actively signals
> you're early on relationships too. What in your backgrounds makes an investor
> believe you specifically will crack this? If the honest answer is 'nothing
> obvious yet,' that's a gap to close before you send this, not a bio to polish."

## Example 5 — Full-deck cold read

> **Context:** 11-slide deck. Order: Vision → Product → Tech Architecture → Team →
> Problem → Market → Traction → Business Model → Competition → Roadmap → Ask ($1.5M).

**❌ FAIL (praise + generic):**
> "Solid deck overall! Good coverage of the key areas. A few things to consider:
> maybe tighten the vision slide, add more detail to traction, and make the ask
> clearer. With some polish this will be in good shape. Nice work!"

**✅ PASS:**
> "**Cold read:** This reads as a strong technical team that's selling the product
> before the problem, so an investor doesn't yet know why to care. The tech is
> clearly real; the *why now* and the *why this matters* are buried.
>
> **Top 3 deal-killers:**
> 1. **Order.** You open with Vision and Product and don't hit the Problem until
>    slide 5. By then a skeptical reader has already decided this is a solution
>    hunting for a problem. The problem has to come before the product.
> 2. **No 'why now.'** Nothing in here says why this is buildable/winnable in 2026
>    and wasn't in 2020. Without it, an investor asks 'why hasn't this been done?'
>    and you have no answer on the page.
> 3. **Ask ≠ milestones.** You ask for $1.5M but never say what it buys or what
>    milestone it gets you to. An investor can't judge if the number is right.
>
> Slide 3 (Tech Architecture) is a classic technical-founder move — it's detail an
> investor doesn't need at pre-seed and it costs you a slide you need for the
> problem. Slides 8–9 do their job; leave them.
>
> **If you fix nothing else:** move Problem to slide 2 and add a real 'why now.'
> Everything else is downstream of that."

## Example 6 — Value proposition (missing ingredients)

> **Slide text:** "Our AI platform helps businesses work smarter and move faster.
> We're building the future of productivity."

**❌ FAIL (rewriter + generic):**
> "Sharpen your value prop. Try: 'For mid-market ops teams drowning in manual
> reporting, our platform cuts reporting time 80% with automated data pipelines.'
> Much clearer!"

*Why it fails:* it writes the value-prop sentence for them — the founder's job — and
invents specifics (mid-market, 80%) that aren't theirs.

**✅ PASS:**
> "I tried to reconstruct your value proposition from this slide and I can't — which
> means an investor can't either. A value prop has to position you against how the
> need is met *today*: for a specific segment, who has a specific need, you deliver a
> specific benefit, by some mechanism, *unlike* the current alternative. Here I see
> none of those — 'businesses,' 'smarter,' 'faster,' 'the future' name no one and
> compare to nothing. Who exactly feels this, what do they use now, and what can you
> do that the alternative can't? Until those are answerable, this reads as a slogan,
> not a proposition — and the whole deck hangs on it."

## Example 7 — Risk / reward (upside only)

> **Slide text:** "$50B market. First mover. 10x better than incumbents. Projected
> $100M ARR by year 4."

**❌ FAIL (praise):**
> "Love the ambition! Big market and a strong first-mover story. Maybe add a source
> for the $50B and you're set."

**✅ PASS:**
> "This slide is all reward and no risk, and a pre-seed investor is trained to
> distrust that — when a founder names no risks, the investor silently supplies them,
> uncharitably. 'First mover' and '10x better' are the two claims incumbents crush
> most often, and there's no line on what would have to go right for $100M ARR to
> land, or how likely that is. What are the two or three things that could actually
> sink this, and what's your honest read on their likelihood? A deck that names its
> risks *and* their likelihood reads as more fundable than one that pretends there
> are none."

## Example 8 — Hard on issues, soft on people (the delivery)

Same finding, two deliveries. The substance is identical; the *voice* is the
difference between bruising the founder and coaching them.

> **Context:** A traction slide implies momentum, but nothing on it shows a single
> paying customer — and the founder confirms there are none yet.

**❌ FAIL (blunt / transactional / mildly blaming):**
> "Since not having clients is a known thing that makes investors turn away, you
> should tell investors somewhere in the pitch that you have no clients. It's just
> about transparency."

*Why it fails:* it's accurate but it lands as a scolding to-do. It assigns fault
("you should tell them"), frames the fact as a liability, and misses the chance to
turn it into a strength.

**✅ PASS (hard on the issue, soft on the person, opportunity-framed):**
> "Right now an investor could reach the end of the pitch still assuming you have
> paying customers — and discovering later that you don't reads as a transparency
> problem, which taints trust in everything else you claimed. That's the issue, and
> it's a real one. But it's also an opening: *if* you name the no-revenue-yet reality
> yourself, early and plainly, you defuse the risk **and** earn the right to show how
> you're closing it — which turns a perceived weakness into a window onto your
> go-to-market thinking. Where in the pitch could you own this on your own terms, and
> what would you want the investor to conclude about your plan when you do?"

*Why it passes:* blunt about the risk (no sugar-coating the issue), generous to the
founder (no blame), reframes the gap as an opportunity, ties it to what the investor
concludes — and still hands back a **question**, never the words.
