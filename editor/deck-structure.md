# Reference — pre-seed deck structure

The standard pre-seed narrative deck and the ONE job each slide must do for an
investor. Use this to name a slide and judge whether it's doing its job. This is
the sequence a skeptical investor expects; deviating from it is allowed only if the
story is *stronger* for it.

## The standard sequence (10–12 slides)

| # | Slide | Its ONE job |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Title / one-liner | Say what you do in one plain sentence a non-expert gets. |
| 2 | Problem | Prove a specific, painful, expensive problem exists for specific people. |
| 3 | Solution | Show how you remove that pain — the insight, not the feature list. |
| 4 | Why now | Explain why this is winnable *now* and wasn't 3 years ago. |
| 5 | Market | Show the market is big enough for a venture outcome — bottoms-up. |
| 6 | Product | Make it real: what it does, ideally shown, not told. |
| 7 | Traction | Give evidence of real pull — the least-gameable number you have. |
| 8 | Business model | Show how money is made and that the unit economics can work. |
| 9 | Competition | Show you know the landscape and have a defensible wedge. |
| 10 | Team | Answer: why is THIS team the one to win THIS market? |
| 11 | Ask + use of funds | State the round size and the milestone it buys. |
| 12 | Vision (optional) | Show the big outcome if it works. |

## What "does its job" means, slide by slide

- **Problem** — passes only if a stranger can name who hurts and how much. "X is
  broken" is not a problem; "Y people lose Z hours/dollars to X" is.
- **Solution** — passes if it conveys the *insight* (why you win), not a feature
  tour. Features are for the product slide.
- **Why now** — the most-skipped slide and often the most important at pre-seed.
  Passes only if it names a real shift (tech, regulatory, behavioral) that opened
  the window.
- **Market** — passes with bottoms-up logic (customers × price). Top-down
  ("$X billion market, we take 1%") fails on sight.
- **Traction** — passes with a qualified, hard-to-game number. At pre-seed this can
  be small; it just has to be *real* and show pull.
- **Team** — passes only if it demonstrates founder-market fit, not enthusiasm.
- **Ask** — passes only if the number is tied to a concrete milestone (e.g., "18
  months to $30K MRR / Series A metrics").
  - **Strategy or Implementation Plan** — passes if it conveys a logical, or proven, theory of change whose assumptions can be cited either in authoritative sources, studies, logic itself where it stands to reason that A causes B causes C, so C depends on B depends on A, which illustrates RISK and whether any mitigation tactics are in place to ensure that sequence will occur. Or it is rooted in the authority of market adoption through revenue, pre-sales, kickstarter, actual clients, prototyping, or piloting. 

## Common ordering mistakes

- **Product/vision before problem.** The reader decides "solution in search of a
  problem" before you make your case. Problem comes early.
- **Why now missing entirely.** Leaves the investor's "why hasn't someone done
  this?" unanswered.
- **Ask buried or absent.** If the investor has to hunt for the number and the
  milestone, the slide failed.
- **Tech architecture as its own slide** at pre-seed. Almost always detail the
  investor doesn't need yet (see `founder-archetypes.md`).

Pre-seed decks can be shorter than this — but every job above still has to be done
*somewhere*. If a job is missing entirely, that's a gap to flag.

## The ten questions every deck must answer

Slides are just the *packaging*; what an investor is really hunting for is answers to
ten questions. Any deck — 5 slides or 15 — has to answer these *somewhere*. Use this
to spot a **missing answer** even when the slide count looks complete. (The
`why now` job above is folded into "the opportunity."). this is NOT A SEQUENCED OUTLINE or TABLE OF CONTENTS for a deck, this is a list of the questions that must be clearly answered after the investor has read the deck. The structure should provide an introduction, some context, a clear description of the business problem, the market that experiences that problem in practical 'on the ground' terms, alternatives to solving that problem, how the founders solution solves the problem, the strategy for market adoption, the differentiator and competitive advantage, the ask from the investor, the team itself and their experience,the risks and rewards that the investor is being asked to take on, and a call to action. While not all pitches have market or user validation, if this is something in the pitch, it would speak to risks and rewards, since market validation reduces the risk that the solution is not adoptable.

| # | Question the deck must answer | Where it usually lives |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Who are you? | Title / Team |
| 2 | What practical, **observable** problem needs solving? *(the key one)* | Problem |
| 3 | What are the current available solutions? | Problem / Competition |
| 4 | What is your solution, and how many use / pay for it? | Solution / Product / Traction |
| 5 | What is your competitive advantage? | Competition / Solution |
| 6 | What is the opportunity (why big, why now)? | Market / Why now |
| 7 | What is your business strategy? | Business model / Ask |
| 8 | Who is your team and how are you equipped to win? | Team |
| 9 | What are the risks, and their likelihood? | Risk framing / Ask |
| 10 | What are the rewards, and their likelihood? | Vision / Ask / Results |

Risks and rewards are **two separate questions**, not one — an investor weighs them
against each other. A deck that shows reward with no risk reads as naïve; a deck that
shows risk with no reward reads as unfundable. Both must be answerable.

If a slide exists but doesn't *answer its question*, that's a job not done. If a
question has no home anywhere in the deck, that's a gap to flag. Question 2 is the
load-bearing one: if the problem isn't observable, everything downstream stands on
air (see `value-proposition-recipe.md`).

## A worked skeleton (a real short-form order)

One proven order for a tight, 5-page-style narrative — useful as a comparator when a
founder's order feels off:

> **Opportunity → Answer → Money → Strategy → Competition → Advantage →
> Capability → Team → Results**

Note it opens with the *opportunity/problem*, not the product, and lands the
*differentiator* ("Advantage") and *evidence* ("Results") near the end. When a deck
leads with product or vision instead, name it (see "Common ordering mistakes").
