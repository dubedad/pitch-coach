# Reference — founder archetypes and their blind spots

Founders make predictable mistakes based on their background. If the founder's
profile is evident from the deck (or they tell you), name the pattern — it lands
harder than a generic note because it shows you've seen this before. Do NOT
stereotype without evidence; use this to sharpen a note you already have grounds for.

## The technical founder

**Strengths on the page:** the product is real, the tech is credible, the demo works.

**Blind spots:**
- **Over-explains the technology.** Architecture diagrams, stack details, and
  how-it-works depth an investor doesn't need at pre-seed — often at the cost of the
  problem and market slides.
- **Under-sells the market and the "why anyone cares."** Assumes the tech speaks for
  itself. It doesn't.
- **Leads with the solution.** Builds first, justifies the problem second.
- **Buries commercial thinking.** Weak or absent business model / go-to-market.

**Sharpened note pattern:** "This is a classic technical-founder move — you're
selling the *how* before the *why anyone cares.* Slide 3's architecture is detail an
investor doesn't need yet; that real estate should go to the problem."

## The business / non-technical founder

**Strengths on the page:** clean narrative, big vision, market framed well.

**Blind spots:**
- **Over-claims market size.** Comfortable with top-down TAM; light on bottoms-up
  reality.
- **Thin on product specifics.** Hand-waves *how* it actually works or gets built.
- **'Technical co-founder: TBD'** or an outsourced build — a real credibility gap at
  pre-seed for a tech company.
- **Buzzword density.** Leans on market language ("AI-powered," "disruptive") in
  place of evidence.

**Sharpened note pattern:** "The narrative is strong, but an investor will poke at
execution: who builds this, and why do you win on product rather than just framing?
Right now that's a gap, not a slide to wordsmith."

## The first-time founder (any background)

**Blind spots:**
- **Asks for too little.** Under-raises and can't hit the milestone the next round
  requires. (Also read: doesn't understand the funding ladder.)
- **No milestone framing on the ask.** Numbers without "what this 18 months buys."
- **Over-polishes minor slides, under-invests in problem and why-now.**
- **Hides weaknesses** instead of addressing them — reads as naive to an experienced
  investor (see the honesty premium in `investor-signals.md`).
- **Mimics a famous deck's format** without its substance.

**Sharpened note pattern:** "The $400K ask is likely too small — what milestone does
it get you to, and is that enough to raise your seed? Investors read an
under-sized ask as not understanding the funding ladder."

## How to use this

- Only invoke an archetype when the deck gives you evidence for it.
- The value is the *specific, been-here-before* framing — pair it with a concrete
  slide reference and the investor read.
- Never use it to excuse a rewrite. Naming the pattern still ends in a question, not
  a fix.
